About Me

Hello! My name is Kathir Ilango and I'm currently a junior at UCLA, studying Computer Science and Engineering. My goal is to be a software developer, and I've been fortunate enough to gain industry experience over the summers through my first three years of college. In 2018 I worked at Avi Networks, a multicloud application delivery company, as an intern writing test suites. In 2019 I worked at Workato, an integration automation company, as a software engineering intern integrating AWS SNS into the company's platform.

Outside of school, I've spent my entire life having a massive passion for sports. I like to play and watch almost all of them, but football is the most special to me. I've grown up a diehard Niners fan and there are very few things in life that I would like to see more than them winning a Super Bowl. I'm also a huge fan of sports analytics. At UCLA, I'm the Journalism Chair of Bruin Sports Analytics, overseeing the production of quarterly sports data science articles written by our members and even writing some articles of my own. I'm a big believer in the power of data analytics and computer science within the realm of sports and hope to make a difference in the field one day.

Projects

Modern NFL Receiver Analysis

This project looks at advanced NFL receiver data and performs a Principal Component Analysis and K-means Clustering to create a model that classifies the receivers into various groups based on their numbers. The idea was to be able to use this to better understand the tiers of modern receivers in the NFL and how they differ from the traditional roles that have been around for years.

View Code

DVOA Analysis

Defense-adjusted Value Over Average is a football efficiency metric that compares the situational success of a given play to league-average results in that situation. This project scrapes DVOA data from the 1985 NFL season to the most recent season and creates a regression model that predicts the number of regular season games a team will win based on its team DVOA numbers.

View Code

Positional Spending Analysis

This project makes a model that determines a correct way for NFL teams to spend their money (or shows that positional spending has nothing to do with winning in the NFL). It scrapes positional spending data for every NFL team dating back to 2013 as well as win/loss data and creates a decision tree classification model that predicts which teams in the current or upcoming NFL season will win at least a certain number of games (specified by the user).
